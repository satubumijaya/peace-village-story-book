INTRODUCTION

On Indonesia’s island of Java, women are essential to overcoming the threat posed by radicalism towards peace and human security at community level. Everyday women – diverse in age and background – are flourishing to become agents for peace, leading their communities to prevent violence, promote tolerance, and improve social cohesion.

These true grassroots leaders navigate deeply rooted patriarchy and gender conservatism, working towards community resilience, equality for all citizens, and real respect for diversity within each of their villages – known as Peace Villages

In the working group itself there are approximately 40% of members who are male, and they remain accepting – and enthusiastic – of such women-led movements in a traditionally patriarchal society. For Hasanah, as a life-long activist, the impact of the Peace Village programme has significant personal meaning, as herself and other women now feel more fulfilled and engaged than ever before.

They then sit with their local government to explore and identify ways to promote tolerance and sustain peace in their village, and push for change in village government policy and processes. This approach, through both community and government, counters the issues of radicalism and intolerance from both ends, fostering understanding, resilience and peaceful enjoyment of life in the village for all.

In particular, it is four Peace Villages in East Java that are piloting approaches established on global human security principles, while maintaining a focus towards resilience within the community.

Supported by UN Women and the Wahid Foundation under the Guyub Project, the villages of Prancak, Sidomulyo, Candirenggo and Guluk-Guluk evidence strong outcomes and impacts stemming from the empowerment and inclusion of women.

Peace Village Action Plans form the foundation of progress in these particular villages, with women leading and contributing to plan development and implementation in their communities. It is the stories of empowerment, inclusion and leadership for the women themselves – agents for peace and promoters of diversity – that truly reflect the meaning and importance of the Indonesian Peace Village.

GULUK-GULUK

VILLAGE

Located in the Sumenep region on Madura Island, Guluk-Guluk village was traditionally known for ongoing community conflict and tensions, often related to local politics.

Through the Peace Village programme, women were engaged in economic empowerment activities that increased their standing and voice in their community, and resulted in successful peacebuilding and conflict resolution efforts led by women in the community.

Women – young and old – are now approaching the village’s political conflicts head-on, and ensuring harmonious and peaceful relationships between all parties throughout Guluk-Guluk village.

Story

01. FADHILAH

“Men and women just never sat at the same table – we were never equal.”

Much of Fadhilah’s life has been spent teaching young girls in one of Guluk-Guluk village’s Islamic boarding schools. Having grown up in the Sumenep region, Fadhilah was accustomed to a patriarchal society and the inequality it causes for Guluk-Guluk’s women on a daily basis.

It was the arrival of the Peace Village programme that began to shift the balance. Although Fadhilah didn’t initially engage in the activities, it was her offer of a place to stay for one of the programme’s facilitators that introduced her to the ideas and opportunities offered by the Peace Village concept.

“When we spoke at my home about the inequality, and how much of it was rooted in a lack of financial independence, I became very interested in the Peace Village idea and its activities.”

Initially, Fadhilah and the other women were provided training on financial literacy and management.

This had a significant impact on how they managed their small home-based businesses.

Women in Guluk-Guluk often undertake household production and sales of goods such as food, drinks and handicrafts.

This aims to provide ongoing additional income to their family unit.

However, for Fadhilah and other women this had previously been more like a hobby that never really provided much profit, until they participated in the financial literacy training provided by Wahid Foundation through the Peace Village programme.

“The understanding and skills that the training provided started significant change for women in our community, as it was practical, and when we used it all of a sudden we started making money. We now actually have some money and financial independence, and the men can see the value in the work we are doing.”

This economic-based increase in equality for the women of Guluk-Guluk was, however, just the beginning of real change. From this starting point, the Peace Village program began to expand its reach, and Fadhilah started to experience a connectedness between the activities that the women undertook and a strengthening in their unity and engagement in the village.

Having begun to realise their economic potential, Guluk-Guluk’s women also started to take on larger roles in village society and politics. Fadhilah joined wider activities as part of the Guluk-Guluk Peace Village working group, and began to witness the increased equality that this working group created through an array of different contexts.

While the increase in equality doesn’t stop at the existence of a specific room in the village government office reserved for the Guluk-Guluk women’s groups, it is representative of the change that has been brought about by the Peace Village initiative.

Growing from the realization of financial independence in the home and the wider community, women such as Fadhilah are now increasingly present, engaged and empowered in many aspects of the village’s society.

Story

02. ISTIBSYARAH

“Getting involved with the Wahid Foundation’s Youth Camp allowed me to make new friends from the city, who have different backgrounds and follow different religions.”

Istibsyarah was aware of certain pockets of intolerance and fanaticism growing amongst the youth of Guluk-Guluk village.

While she didn’t feel that she was particularly intolerant herself, Istibsyarah wanted to experience more diversity outside of her village.

The Peace Village programme began engaging the youth of Guluk-Guluk in an array of activities.

So Istibsyarah was quick to join in with her friends, jumping into youth-orientated activities that focused on tolerance and inter-faith harmony.

“Since meeting new friends who also have different religions, I am much more interested in diversity and tolerance, and more understanding of different ideas, beliefs and opinions.”

The Peace Village programme in Guluk-Guluk invited the village’s youth to come together in activities and forums to broaden their understanding and interaction across backgrounds, religious beliefs, and differing age groups.

These types of activities were significantly different than those that Istibsyarah would usually get involved with, but she was quick to leave behind any feelings of hesitancy or awkwardness.

The camps and challenges included young women and men from Guluk-Guluk, and delivered training and support on how to overcome issues faced by the region’s youth, including intolerance, harassment and online gender-based violence.

“I was never particularly involved with adults in our village, but now I am confident to engage in community-based activities with all members of our village.”

Aside from delving into youth-based issues and outcomes, Guluk-Guluk’s Peace Village working group is also linking youth with the wider village social structures – as well as with formal village institutions and governance. Through the programme, the village’s youth have been empowered to deliver their specific input and ideas within village forums and sessions, ensuring that their needs and voices are considered within wider village decisions and activities.

As a result, the adults of Guluk-Guluk are increasingly enthusiastic to engage with and invite the input of the community’s youth. While it is still early days, more youth are now included in the Peace Village working group, and it is hoped this will bring about more positive outcomes for Guluk-Guluk’s younger generations.

Istibsyarah’s life, and the lives of many other of the village’s youth, have changed since the implementation of the Peace Village programme in Guluk-Guluk. The youth-focused activities have supported them to break from the everyday norms of village life and take part in something exciting and new.

Istibsyarah and her friends – old and new – hope that these activities can expand and develop to address more challenges and engage more youth, and that they can all play an active role in the creation of younger generations founded on tolerance and diversity.

Story

03. HASANAH

“While it began as economic empowerment activities, the Peace Village idea has now broadened into a variety of community-led efforts aimed at creating a more conducive environment for everyone to enjoy life in Guluk-Guluk.”

Hasanah has always been active in the community, engaging with environmental efforts and women’s farming groups in Guluk-Guluk village for many years. These experiences saw her quickly take on a leadership role when Peace Village activities began in 2017, as she recognised the value in the economic empowerment focus that the programme offered to women in her community.

Over the years the programme broadened its scope – introducing Hasanah to new people and ideas – with and her skills and experience as a community leader then developed and utilised to play a key role in conflict management and peacebuilding efforts in the wider community.

“In the Peace Village activities I learnt how to better handle conflict, and how to focus on building peace and harmony between people in my community. How I should respond to problems as they arise was a big part of what I learnt.”

Village elections have traditionally been a time of high tension and significant conflict in Guluk-Guluk.

Hasanah and the Peace Village working group took action to de-escalate rising tensions in the community during the lead-up to the most recent ballot.

They convened meetings and discussions between the competing candidates and their supporters.

Finally, all key parties signed an agreement of peace and good behaviour that would be adhered to during the election period.

As a result, the elections ran smoothly and peacefully for the first time in many years, and the outcomes were accepted and supported by all parties involved in the mediation, as well as in the wider community.

As a Board Member for the Peace Village working group, Hasanah and her team are working to expand the reach of their peacebuilding efforts. They have developed Kompolan Esto Damai – which is a forum for community participation in the village, and also a mechanism for early identification of conflict.

In the working group itself there are approximately 40% of members who are male, and they remain accepting – and enthusiastic – of such women-led movements in a traditionally patriarchal society. For Hasanah, as a life-long activist, the impact of the Peace Village programme has significant personal meaning, as herself and other women now feel more fulfilled and engaged than ever before.

“The women of Guluk-Guluk have become more creative. After they engage as a group, often for the first time in their lives, I can see this new type of happiness emerge. Maybe because they meet other women, and when they get together new ideas continue to appear.”

PRANCAK

VILLAGE

For women in Prancak village, in Madura island’s Sumenep region, patriarchy has often hindered their engagement in social and political life.

The establishment of strong women’s groups through the Peace Village programme formed a key driver for change, supporting women to take on increasing responsibility and leadership across Prancak village and its institutions.

Now, women take leading roles in their neighbourhoods and village groups, working towards ending domestic violence and increasing protection for women and children in Prancak.

Story

04. MARIA ULFA

“People in the neighbourhood looked down on me because I was a woman leaving the village, but all I wanted to do was get my Bachelor degree.”

Maria Ulfa is the only woman in her hamlet of almost 600 people who has a university degree. Once her degree had finished, however, she was still forced to marry by her family not long after graduation, virtually ending her hopes of continuing on to study her Masters.

As was the norm in her village of Prancak, she was forced to come to terms with the belief that her role was to continue life as a housewife and mother. It was in this new context that Maria Ulfa began to witness more and more cases of domestic violence and sexual assault taking place in the community, compelling her to act, regardless of community opinion.

“Women don’t want to report violence or sexual assault because reporting these things is seen as shameful. But I couldn’t just turn a blind eye, so I always got involved.”

Maria Ulfa joined the Peace Village programme with an interest in supporting survivors of violence and sexual assault.

She was surprised to find out that women and children have considerable government protection, but it is just not realized in her village.

Maria Ulfa participated in Peace Village training on supporting survivors of violence, and determining the best options for protection.

She now uses such approaches in her ongoing efforts to support other women in Prancak.

I now know where to take women who have been abused, and who is the best party to report to. Because this is a small community, lots of people don’t like me because I get involved, but don’t care, because I just want to protect the women and children.”

Building on the work that Maria Ulfa and her women’s group have been undertaking through the Peace Village programme, they are now being supported to develop the Women and Child Protection Unit, which was recently established in Prancak village.

At this early stage the group participates in training and activities that provide them with increased knowledge and practice in protection measures, that can later be utilized in their work with the community’s women and children.

Maria Ulfa hopes that this – and other activities delivered through the Peace Village programme – can continue to improve the context for women by empowering them to support each other and their communities.

Story

05. ARIFAH

“I had heard of the Peace Village concept, but never really understood what it meant. However, once I got involved, I learnt many things that were new and interesting, and that I had never really considered.”

The influence of the Peace Village programme in Arifah’s village of Prancak is significant. It was the programme’s activities that prepared and drove Arifah to be more engaged in her community, resulting in her being chosen as the head of her neighbourhood – a position often reserved for men.

In a region home to high levels of child marriage and early marriage, Arifah is utilizing this leadership to counter the issue; focusing on education for girls, as well as direct protection for women and children.

“Many of these issues can be overcome by parents understanding the rights and opportunities for their girls”.

As one of her first acts in the position of neighbourhood head, Arifah organized parenting classes for households in her community.

Delivered under Peace Village programming, these classes proved beneficial for both the parents and their children.

They provide simple and culturally appropriate guidance on gender and children’s education for Prancak’s parents.

The community are recognizing the importance of school for their girls, and many now want to prioritize their children’s education.

“So many parents in our village have joined the classes, and they always speak about the value of the new knowledge and skills they receive.”

After being chosen as the neighbourhood head, Arifah was also chosen to lead Prancak’s Women and Child Protection Unit, implemented through the Peace Village programme.

She continues to develop and expand the unit, and has also advocated to the district Regent for its inclusion within formal policy and programming at the village level across the wider region.

These roles see Arifah not only leading the women of Prancak village, but also numerous men, in what is a traditionally patriarchal society.

Arifah credits the softer and more inclusive approach to her interactions with the community that she learnt through Peace Village training and workshops as the key to navigating some of the gender-based challenges in her new positions of leadership.

As Arifah witnesses the change experienced by Prancak’s women, she also reflects on her own personal growth. From farming rice to representing her community in front of male-dominated government forums, Arifah has experienced personal change on many levels.

She also works with others to experience such change, as more and more women become increasingly active in the community – empowered to do so through the Peace Village activities.

Arifah also wants women in her village to be part of whatever activity, career, or pathway they choose for themselves, and is working to establish a women’s forum to support members to explore these aspirations.

“I’ve learnt to use my voice. I’ve learnt to advocate respectfully and softly, but to always have a voice. Now, I have confidence, as a woman, to use this voice.”

SIDOMULYO

VILLAGE

On the outskirts of Batu City, Sidomulyo village has often faced issues for women and children related to poverty and low levels of education for many of its residents.

By engaging women and youth, the Peace Village programme focused towards directly protecting women and children, while also approaching the causes of community conflict, including working with households to generate income rather than taking loans.

Women are central to the Peace Village efforts that include income development training, as well as protection and improvement of education opportunities for Sidomulyo’s children.

Story

06. AZIZAH

“I have always helped children who need me, but after engaging in the Peace Village process I am now more prepared to expand and develop the support I can provide.”

A teacher at Sidomulyo’s local primary school, Azizah holds a lifelong interest in the welfare and development of the village’s children. Such an interest saw her participate in the Peace Village programme during its early years, and has resulted in Azizah becoming a leader of women and child protection efforts in Sidomulyo village.

As the Secretary of the Sidomulyo Peace Village Working Group Azizah is effecting positive change for women and children across the village, while in her own newly developed programme she provides hands-on support for a number of children who are neglected or abandoned.

Azizah implements the Rumah Ayom programme from her own home, supporting a number of children from Sidomulyo.

The programme provides a place of refuge and safety for children who have been neglected or abandoned.

Such issues are due to parents who have divorced, migrated for work, or given birth out of wedlock in the prominent conservative culture.

Rumah Ayom provides the children a safe place to learn, rest and develop, while also focusing on preventative actions to improve the situation.

Many triggers of child neglect in Sidomulyo are financial, and in her role with the Peace Village working group Azizah also focuses on guiding parents through activities aimed to improve livelihoods.

She hopes that through these actions parents can become more capable and prepared to take responsibility for their children.

More broadly, financial issues are also a key cause of conflict between community members in Sidomulyo, and Azizah has utilized the Peace Village forums to engage the community regarding debt and borrowing between households.

With increased awareness on debt avoidance, community members are then invited to trainings and activities on work skills development and financial management.

“I basically tell them don’t go into debt, go to work and make the money that you need – and that’s where we provide the activities to help them improve their incomes.”

Whether related to children, or conflict and issues in the wider community, Azizah has witnessed significant change over recent years.

She says that the issue of debt is almost non-existent, people are increasing and expanding their working skills, and citizens are genuinely living more peacefully within a stronger community atmosphere.

While the challenge for protecting women and children remains, Azizah also hopes that interventions such as her Rumah Ayom can be replicated and refined to ensure an even safer environment for all.

She has also witnessed a change in herself, and her capacity to approach conflict and issues in a more peaceful and relaxed manner.

“I feel as if I am wiser in handling conflict. I don’t take issues to heart, and when things don’t go my way I don’t get so upset. I feel very peaceful.”

Story

07. MEY & DEWI

“The Peace Village has changed my mindset. I used to want to just be average – not stand out. Now I want to act fairly and be a decision-maker, and I now know who I am as a woman and a young person.”

- Dewi

Mey and Dewi were both in university when they first got involved in the Peace Village programme taking place in their village of Sidomulyo. After being active in the Youth Camp and other Peace Village activities, both women began utilizing their newfound skils, understanding and confidence to enact change in the community around them.

“I learnt so much about handling conflict and being more tolerant. I realized that I had to be at peace with myself first, then I could work for peace in my environment.”
– Mey

Their experience growing up in Sidomulyo was to shape the way that Mey and Dewi approached issues faced by women in their community. Many of the community members still see a women’s role as only in the home, and this was a belief that Dewi and Mey wanted to change.

To do this, they felt that they had to start changing the perceptions held by Sidomulyo’s women themselves. Through the Peace Village Challenge, the young women proposed, and successfully implemented, a workshop called Human Rights School, which aimed to provide the women of Sidomulyo with an array of information and ideas regarding their rights and opportunities.

“Young women here are so often underestimated, but through Peace Village we were equal with the men and our input was valued. This was the idea upon which we developed the Human Rights School project.” - Dewi

Mey and Dewi noticed an increase in conflict between children and their parents – particularly mothers – due to increased time at home during the pandemic in 2020.

They again turned to the Peace Village Challenge, this time to support alternative learning opportunities for the village’s children.

The Learning Garden project invited youth to learn outside of their classrooms and homes, and to discover that learning doesn’t have to be monotone.

Children engaged with the natural environment, and also learnt about things such as human rights and tolerance.

“The children were happier, more relaxed, and friendly towards the teachers and their friends around them. It was a really significant change just from a small environmental adjustment.” - Mey

Since their engagement in the Peace Village programme, the situation is beginning to change in the Sidomulyo community.

The support of children’s wellbeing is being expanded and strengthened through Rumah Ayom, while women are taking a much more proactive role in handling conflict and building tolerance in the community through the Peace Village working group.

For Mey and Dewi, the inclusion and engagement of youth – as the seeds of the nation’s future – is imperative for peaceful and tolerant Sidomulyo, as well as for Indonesia as a whole.

CANDIRENGGO

VILLAGE

Just north of Malang City, Candirenggo village has been constantly challenged to balance its diversity with its citizen’s more traditional beliefs and practices.

To counter an increase in conflict and disharmony over recent years, the Peace Village programme has empowered women in the community to become leaders and drivers of peace and tolerance at the village level.

Women’s groups have developed safe spaces for women to have a more profound voice in village affairs, while youth have also become more central to peacebuilding efforts, resulting in a more inclusive and harmonious environment for all community members in Candirenggo.

Story

08. SENDY

“The most meaningful thing I have learnt is that women form a strong bridge for positive communication throughout our community.”

It was the experience of a neighbour that drove Sendy to action supporting women in her village of Candirenggo. When the neighbour’s husband was arrested on terrorism charges, she was hardly sighted outside of her home – too embarrassed to engage with the rest of the community.

“She was really finding things hard because of this. She had no money, no means of support, but she also didn’t want to help herself as she felt such shame due to her husband’s situation.”

Sendy and her women’s group stepped in to help by including her neighbour in their activities, and slowly bringing her back into the community. Through the Peace Village initiative in Candirenggo, the women were offered financing for their home businesses, and Sendy’s neighbour was included within such targeted support.

After re-engaging with the community, Sendy explains that the group has worked with her neighbour to the point she is now an independent and active community member once more.

Through peacebuilding activities delivered through the Peace Village programme, Sendy and her community also drove the establishment of a working group for handling cases of violence towards women and children, as well as a village hotline for those in danger of violence.

“With increased engagement and opportunity, women became more confident to take action regarding issues such as domestic violence.”

This action was appearing in varying forms.

Through the working group, women realized that taking care of domestic violence issues could be successful at the community level.

Women’s groups were also invited to engage in formal forums and meetings undertaken by the village government.

These results culminated in the opening of the Ayu Candira Gallery; a community space for women’s empowerment in Candirenggo village.

Step-by-step, the role of women in the village took on greater meaning, and quickly became a valuable stream for communication between the community and its government representatives.

The Peace Village’s peacebuilding and livelihoods activities have provided a foundation upon which Sendy and the other women of Candirenggo can continue to grow.

Opportunities for the community’s women will only continue to emerge when they are empowered to become more independent, as has resulted from the programme’s efforts within the village neighbourhoods.

As challenges will always arise, the women of Candirenggo aim to ensure sustainability for their women’s group, regardless of changing political or social contexts. It is this secure, safe and empowering space that Sendy envisages for all the women of Candirenggo and its surrounds.

Story

09. HALIMAH

“I just want our community to live together
– in peace.”

Halimah joined a local neighbourhood women’s group to be more involved outside of her home in the community, and to take part in livelihood training which could support her small cake business. The group began as a space to learn about opportunities for improving economic outcomes, and has transformed into a place for the women of Candirenggo village, such as Halimah, to play an active role in supporting and developing their wider community.

“Most of us have traditionally just worked taking care of our homes, so I really wanted to be part of something more. I wanted to grow.”

Initial activities in the Karya Wiguna Gunda women’s group focused on handicraft, sewing and cooking.

Halimah recognised the value of such an opportunity for women to increase their contribution to their household finances.

Economic empowerment was only the beginning for the group, with the women then approaching other community challenges.

They now approach domestic violence and other household issues through their tight-knit group through support from the Peace Village programme.

“Sometimes there can be a lot of problems between women in the village. But our women’s group now gathers often, and we know each other better, so conflict between women has decreased.”

The group provides Halimah and others a space in which to have a voice, and also a place to turn if things get difficult. Previously, issues for women could often be amplified and protracted as news leaked out into the wider community.

The establishment of the women’s group through the Peace Village programme countered this by providing direct and relevant support for whatever problems may arise. The group also gives victims of domestic violence somewhere to turn for help, and Halimah and her friends are ready to support.

“So many of the women have increased self-confidence. We are always invited to activities and discussions in the village now, as our input is valued, and we have the confidence to deliver our ideas in front of everyone.”

More than just a training and support mechanism, the group is now actively engaged on shaping outcomes for Halimah and other women in her neighbourhood. They are increasingly taking part in village planning and decision-making activities, and the impact of this on the women of Candirenggo is significant.

As for Halimah, her hopes are simple – for her, her family and the community of Candirenggo.

“We hope to just live in peace with our families, without the problem of domestic violence. And even if there are problems, we want to deal with them properly to avoid further issues.”

Story

10. ANDROMEDA

“After participating in Peace Village activities I have become very engaged in championing women’s rights and equality, and I have realized that I am a feminist.”

After finishing university in 2018, Andromeda felt a little unsure of what came next. For young women such as her in Candirenggo village, career and lifestyle opportunities are sometimes limited, requiring women to either leave and try life in a larger city, or settle down and start making a family.

It was at these crossroads that Andromeda – or Andro as she prefers to be called – was invited to participate in the Peace Village activities being implemented in her district. Andro and two of her friends were selected to join the Youth Camp, and it was this participation that led to her becoming one of Candirenggo’s leading young voices on women’s issues.

Many of Candirenggo’s youth don’t engage in community activities as they are busy working, leaving a gap between the youth and the wider community.

Through the Peace Village Challenge activity, Andro proposed a project about mental health for women and children during the pandemic.

The two-day event was met with positive feedback and appreciation from the women of Candirenggo who participated.

“Young women here are so often underestimated, but through Peace Village we were equal with the men and our input was valued. This was the idea upon which we developed the Human Rights School project.”

The further Andro and her friends engage with their community, the more opportunities they identify to support other women and youth. More recently, with Peace Village support, they have launched a social media campaign for Candirenggo named Local Female Project (@localfemaleproject).

The social media accounts address an array of challenges faced by Candirenggo’s women, including ending domestic violence and countering misinformation, as well as profiling other women leaders from around their community.

“I’ve learnt about our rights in our community – that we actually have a say. I’ve also learnt about how our village works, and the processes I can use to be more actively engaged and improve women’s rights in my community.”

Women’s involvement in village activities in Candirenggo has increased significantly, and it is youth such as Andro who wish to be more actively engaged. Peace Village has brought about change to the way in which village processes are undertaken, ensuring a stronger focus to equality and participation of women and youth.

Increasing the participation of youth at the village level is Andro’s priority, as to ensure that it isn’t the same continuous voices, and that there is refreshment and regeneration brought by a diverse and engaged youth.

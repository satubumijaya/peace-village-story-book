01. FADHILAH

“Men and women just never sat at the same table – we were never equal.”

Much of Fadhilah’s life has been spent teaching young girls in one of Guluk-Guluk village’s Islamic boarding schools. Having grown up in the Sumenep region, Fadhilah was accustomed to a patriarchal society and the inequality it causes for Guluk-Guluk’s women on a daily basis.

It was the arrival of the Peace Village programme that began to shift the balance. Although Fadhilah didn’t initially engage in the activities, it was her offer of a place to stay for one of the programme’s facilitators that introduced her to the ideas and opportunities offered by the Peace Village concept.

“When we spoke at my home about the inequality, and how much of it was rooted in a lack of financial independence, I became very interested in the Peace Village idea and its activities.”

Initially, Fadhilah and the other women were provided training on financial literacy and management.

This had a significant impact on how they managed their small home-based businesses.

Women in Guluk-Guluk often undertake household production and sales of goods such as food, drinks and handicrafts.

This aims to provide ongoing additional income to their family unit.

However, for Fadhilah and other women this had previously been more like a hobby that never really provided much profit, until they participated in the financial literacy training provided by Wahid Foundation through the Peace Village programme.

“The understanding and skills that the training provided started significant change for women in our community, as it was practical, and when we used it all of a sudden we started making money. We now actually have some money and financial independence, and the men can see the value in the work we are doing.”

This economic-based increase in equality for the women of Guluk-Guluk was, however, just the beginning of real change. From this starting point, the Peace Village program began to expand its reach, and Fadhilah started to experience a connectedness between the activities that the women undertook and a strengthening in their unity and engagement in the village.

Having begun to realise their economic potential, Guluk-Guluk’s women also started to take on larger roles in village society and politics. Fadhilah joined wider activities as part of the Guluk-Guluk Peace Village working group, and began to witness the increased equality that this working group created through an array of different contexts.

While the increase in equality doesn’t stop at the existence of a specific room in the village government office reserved for the Guluk-Guluk women’s groups, it is representative of the change that has been brought about by the Peace Village initiative.

Growing from the realization of financial independence in the home and the wider community, women such as Fadhilah are now increasingly present, engaged and empowered in many aspects of the village’s society.
